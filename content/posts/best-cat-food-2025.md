# Best Cat Food 2025: Wet, Dry & Raw Diet Options for Your Cat

Cats are obligate carnivores — meaning they require nutrients found only in animal tissue and cannot thrive on plant-based protein the way omnivores can. This fundamental biology should shape every cat food decision you make. The best cat food in 2025 prioritizes high-quality animal protein, adequate moisture, appropriate fat content, and the specific nutrients (like taurine and arachidonic acid) that cats cannot synthesize themselves.

We analyzed dozens of cat food brands and formulas to bring you the most comprehensive, science-backed guide to cat nutrition in 2025.

## The Case for Wet Food

Before diving into specific products, it's worth addressing what veterinary nutritionists broadly agree on: **cats should eat wet food as their primary diet when possible.**

Cats evolved from desert ancestors and have a naturally low thirst drive — they're designed to get most of their moisture from prey. Domestic cats fed exclusively dry kibble often live in a state of chronic mild dehydration, which over time contributes to urinary tract problems, kidney disease, and constipation. Wet food's high moisture content (70–80%) is more biologically appropriate and actively supports kidney and urinary health.

That said, dry food is more convenient and better for dental health (the chewing action helps reduce tartar). A mixed approach — primarily wet food with some dry for convenience and dental benefit — is what most vets recommend.

## What Cats Need (and Don't)

**Protein:** Should be the primary macronutrient, from named animal sources (chicken, turkey, salmon, tuna). Aim for 30%+ protein on a dry matter basis.

**Taurine:** An amino acid essential for heart function, vision, and reproduction that cats cannot synthesize. Must be present in the food. Virtually all commercial cat foods are taurine-supplemented.

**Arachidonic acid:** An omega-6 fatty acid cats cannot make from plant precursors. Must come from animal fat.

**Vitamin A:** Cats cannot convert beta-carotene to vitamin A — it must come as preformed vitamin A from animal sources.

**Low carbohydrates:** Cats don't need carbohydrates and have limited ability to digest starch. Most high-quality cat foods minimize carb content.

**What to avoid:** Grains in excessive amounts, unnamed "meat" or "animal" ingredients, artificial preservatives (BHA, BHT, ethoxyquin), added sugars, carrageenan (a common thickener linked to inflammation).

## Transitioning to a New Food

Switching cat foods abruptly often causes digestive upset. Transition over 7–10 days: days 1–3, mix 25% new food with 75% old; days 4–6, 50/50; days 7–9, 75% new / 25% old; day 10, fully switched.

If your cat refuses the new food entirely for more than 24–48 hours, don't force it — cats can develop hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver disease) from not eating. Offer their previous food and try a slower transition.

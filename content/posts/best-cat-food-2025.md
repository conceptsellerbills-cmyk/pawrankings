# Best Cat Food in 2025: Wet and Dry Options for Every Cat

Cats are obligate carnivores — their nutritional requirements are fundamentally different from dogs. They require specific nutrients (taurine, arachidonic acid, vitamin A) that must come from animal sources. Understanding this distinction is the foundation of good cat nutrition.

## Wet vs. Dry: The Core Debate

Veterinary nutritionists increasingly recommend wet food as the primary diet for cats. Here's why:

**Cats have a low thirst drive** evolved from their desert-dwelling ancestors, who obtained most moisture from prey. Dry food is typically 8-10% moisture; wet food is 70-80%. Cats on dry-only diets are chronically mildly dehydrated, which contributes to urinary tract disease and kidney problems over time.

**Best nutritional approach:** Wet food as the primary diet, supplemented with dry food if needed for dental health or practical reasons. At minimum, incorporate wet food daily.

## Ingredients to Look For

- **Named animal protein first:** Chicken, salmon, beef — not "meat" or "poultry"
- **High protein:** Minimum 30% dry matter for adults, 35%+ for kittens
- **Taurine:** Essential amino acid cats cannot synthesize — must be present in the food
- **Limited carbohydrates:** Cats have no nutritional requirement for carbohydrates

## Ingredients to Avoid

- Vague proteins: "meat meal," "poultry by-product meal"
- Excessive carbohydrate fillers: corn, wheat, rice as primary ingredients
- Artificial preservatives: BHA, BHT, ethoxyquin
- Added sugars or sweeteners

---

## How Much to Feed Your Cat

Cats are grazers by nature but do well on scheduled meals (2x daily for adults). Free-feeding dry food often leads to overeating and obesity.

Use the food package's guidelines as a starting point, then adjust based on your cat's body condition. You should feel your cat's ribs with gentle pressure but not see them prominently.

## Why Choosing the Right Dog Crate Matters

A dog crate is one of the most important purchases you'll make for your pet — and one of the most misunderstood. Used correctly, a crate isn't a punishment; it's a den. Dogs are naturally den animals, and a properly introduced crate gives them a secure, enclosed space they can call their own. The result is a calmer dog, easier housetraining, safer travel, and fewer destructive behaviors when you're not home.

The problem is that the crate market is full of products that look fine in product photos but fail in real-world use: flimsy welds that bend under pressure, trays that crack and leak, latches that pop open, and sizing that's either too tight or so oversized that the dog loses the sense of enclosure that makes crates work for training.

## Size Guide: Is the 36-Inch iCrate Right for Your Dog?

Getting crate size right is critical. The crate should be large enough for your dog to stand up without hunching, turn around completely, and lie down stretched out — but not so large that a puppy can use one corner as a bathroom and sleep in the other.

The 36-inch iCrate fits dogs up to approximately **24 inches at the shoulder** when fully grown. Interior dimensions are approximately **36"L × 23"W × 25"H**. For reference:

- **Labrador Retriever** (55–80 lbs) — fits, use 36" for females and lighter males
- **Golden Retriever** (55–75 lbs) — fits most females comfortably
- **Australian Shepherd** (40–65 lbs) — excellent fit
- **Siberian Husky** (35–60 lbs) — fits well
- **Boxer** (50–70 lbs) — fits most females and lighter males
- **Border Collie** (30–55 lbs) — ideal fit

For dogs above 70 lbs or breeds that tend toward the larger end of their range (male Labs, German Shepherds, Rottweilers), MidWest's 42-inch or 48-inch iCrate variants are the appropriate step up.

## The Divider Panel: One Crate for the Full Life of Your Dog

The included **metal divider panel** is one of the most practically valuable features on the 36-inch iCrate, especially for puppy owners. Housetraining works on the principle that dogs won't soil the area where they sleep. If the crate is too large, a puppy will simply use one end as a toilet — defeating the purpose entirely.

The divider panel lets you **block off the back portion of the crate**, giving your puppy only as much space as they currently need. As they grow, you slide the panel back. By the time they're fully grown, you remove the panel entirely and they have the full 36 inches.

This means one crate purchase covers your dog from 8 weeks old through their adult life — rather than buying a small puppy crate and then replacing it when they grow out of it. For medium-large breeds, this saves the cost of a second crate and eliminates the transition stress of moving to a new enclosure mid-training.

## Build Quality: Wire Gauge, Welds & Latches

<img src="https://m.media-amazon.com/images/I/81zERyRfAcL._AC_SL1500_.jpg" alt="MidWest iCrate latch mechanism and wire construction close-up" style="width:100%;max-width:700px;display:block;margin:24px auto;border-radius:12px" />

Wire crate quality comes down to three things: wire gauge, weld integrity, and latch security. The iCrate delivers on all three.

**Wire gauge:** The iCrate uses heavier-gauge wire than most crates in its price range. The practical result is that the panels don't flex or bow under pressure from a dog leaning against them, and the corners stay square after repeated folding and unfolding.

**Welds:** Each wire intersection is welded, not just crimped. Crimped connections loosen with use; welds don't. This matters most at the stress points — door hinges, latch attachment points, and the bottom corners where a dog's weight is concentrated.

**Slide-bolt latches:** The single door uses a double slide-bolt latch mechanism that requires deliberate two-handed operation to open. Most dogs cannot manipulate slide bolts the way they can push through spring-clip or single-bolt closures. For escape-prone dogs, this is the latch design you want.
